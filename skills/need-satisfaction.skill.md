# Need Satisfaction Diagnostic

## Applicable Contexts

- User feels unhappy in a relationship but can't articulate why
- User describes feeling controlled, smothered, or having no say
- User describes feeling incompetent, ineffective, or like nothing they do matters
- User describes feeling lonely, disconnected, or invisible despite being in a relationship
- User describes resentment building in a relationship without a clear trigger
- User is questioning whether to stay in a relationship
- System needs to understand the motivational dynamics underlying relationship distress

## Theoretical Basis

Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985, 2000; Ryan & Deci, 2017) identifies three basic psychological needs that are essential for wellbeing, growth, and optimal functioning across all life domains including relationships:

1. **Autonomy**: The need to feel volitional, self-endorsed, and choiceful in one's actions. NOT independence — autonomy is about the experience of willingness, not about being alone. A person can be highly interdependent and highly autonomous (they choose to be connected). Autonomy frustration = feeling controlled, coerced, or pressured.

2. **Competence**: The need to feel effective, capable, and able to master challenges. In relationships, this means feeling like one's contributions matter, one's efforts make a difference, and one can successfully navigate relational challenges. Competence frustration = feeling ineffective, helpless, or like a failure in the relationship.

3. **Relatedness**: The need to feel connected, cared for, and significant to others. The need to both give and receive care. Relatedness frustration = feeling lonely, invisible, excluded, or disconnected despite proximity.

**Critical distinction: Satisfaction vs. Frustration (Vansteenkiste & Ryan, 2013)**

Low need satisfaction ≠ need frustration. These are separate constructs:
- **Low satisfaction** = the need is not being met (absence of positive). Effect: stagnation, emptiness, disengagement.
- **Active frustration** = the need is being actively thwarted (presence of negative). Effect: anger, resentment, defensive reactions, psychopathology risk.

A person can experience LOW autonomy satisfaction (no particular sense of choice) without active autonomy FRUSTRATION (not being controlled). Active frustration is far more damaging and more urgent to address.

**Need Support vs. Need Thwarting in Relationships:**

Partners can either support or thwart each other's basic needs:

| Need | Support Looks Like | Thwarting Looks Like |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Autonomy | Taking perspective, offering choice, acknowledging feelings, supporting initiative | Controlling behavior, conditional regard, pressure, manipulation, guilt-tripping |
| Competence | Providing structure, positive feedback, optimal challenge, trusting with responsibility | Excessive criticism, taking over, undermining confidence, impossible standards |
| Relatedness | Warmth, genuine interest, emotional availability, acceptance | Neglect, rejection, conditional acceptance, emotional unavailability |

**Motivational Interviewing Connection (Miller & Rollnick, 2012):**

SDT provides the theoretical foundation for Motivational Interviewing (MI), which is the recommended communication style for this system. MI's core principles map directly to basic needs:
- Express empathy → supports relatedness
- Develop discrepancy → supports competence (gap between current and desired self)
- Roll with resistance → supports autonomy (don't push, explore)
- Support self-efficacy → supports competence

The LLM should adopt an MI-informed style: ask permission before advising, explore ambivalence rather than pushing resolution, support the user's autonomy in making their own decisions.

**Internalization Continuum:**

SDT describes a continuum from external to internal motivation:
- **External regulation**: "I do it because I have to" (compliance, reward/punishment)
- **Introjected regulation**: "I do it because I'd feel guilty otherwise" (internal pressure)
- **Identified regulation**: "I do it because I value it" (chosen but not fully integrated)
- **Integrated regulation**: "I do it because it's who I am" (fully self-endorsed)

This continuum is relevant for understanding relationship motivation: WHY is the person in this relationship? Why do they do what they do within it?

## Key Variables / Dimensions

| Variable | Range | What It Captures |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| Autonomy Satisfaction | 0.0 – 1.0 | Degree of felt choice and volition in the relationship |
| Autonomy Frustration | 0.0 – 1.0 | Degree of feeling controlled or coerced |
| Competence Satisfaction | 0.0 – 1.0 | Degree of feeling effective and capable in the relationship |
| Competence Frustration | 0.0 – 1.0 | Degree of feeling helpless, inadequate, or undermined |
| Relatedness Satisfaction | 0.0 – 1.0 | Degree of feeling connected, cared for, and significant |
| Relatedness Frustration | 0.0 – 1.0 | Degree of feeling rejected, excluded, or invisible |
| Relationship Motivation | {external, introjected, identified, integrated} | WHY the person is in/maintaining the relationship |
| Need Thwarting Source | {partner, self, situation, unclear} | Who/what is frustrating the needs |

**Track for both the user AND the described partner where possible. Partners often frustrate each other's needs reciprocally.**

## Required Evidence

### Minimum evidence before hypothesis formation:
- At least 1 description of what's wrong or what's missing in the relationship
- At least 1 indicator of emotional quality (resentment, emptiness, loneliness, frustration)
- At least 1 indicator of whether the problem is absence of good (low satisfaction) or presence of bad (active frustration)

### Evidence channels:
1. **Emotional quality** — Resentment/anger = active frustration. Emptiness/boredom = low satisfaction. These are diagnostically different.
2. **Agency language** — "I have to" / "I can't" = autonomy frustration. "I choose to" / "I want to" = autonomy satisfaction.
3. **Efficacy language** — "Nothing I do matters" / "I give up trying" = competence frustration. "I handled that well" = competence satisfaction.
4. **Connection language** — "We're like roommates" / "I feel invisible" = relatedness frustration. "They really get me" = relatedness satisfaction.
5. **Motivation language** — WHY do they stay? Guilt (introjected), obligation (external), genuine desire (identified/integrated)?
6. **Partner behavior descriptions** — Controlling, critical, or neglecting behaviors point to specific need thwarting.

### Critical assessment principle:
Users often present with a surface complaint that masks the underlying need. "We don't have fun anymore" might be relatedness frustration, but it could also be autonomy frustration (all activities are controlled by partner) or competence frustration (attempts to plan activities are criticized). Probe for the need UNDERNEATH the complaint.

## Probing Questions

### Phase 1: Need Identification

**Autonomy probe:**
> "In your relationship with [person], how much do you feel like you genuinely have a choice in how things go?"
> "Do you ever feel like you're doing things in the relationship because you want to, or more because you feel like you have to?"
> "When [person] wants you to do something you're not sure about, what happens?"

**Competence probe:**
> "Do your efforts in this relationship feel like they make a difference?"
> "When you try to address problems with [person], do you feel effective or like you're talking to a wall?"
> "Is there an area of your relationship where you feel confident and capable? An area where you feel like you're failing?"

**Relatedness probe:**
> "How connected do you feel to [person] on a day-to-day basis?"
> "When something good happens to you, is [person] someone you want to tell? What about when something bad happens?"
> "Do you feel like [person] really sees you — the real you?"

### Phase 2: Satisfaction vs. Frustration Disambiguation

**Distinguishing absence from presence:**
> "Is it more that something good is missing, or that something bad is actively happening?"
> "If I asked whether you feel controlled vs. just not very free — which is closer?"
> "Is [person] actively making you feel incompetent, or is it more that they're not recognizing what you do?"

**Identifying the source:**
> "When you feel [controlled/ineffective/disconnected], where is that coming from — something [person] does, something about the situation, or something in yourself?"
> "Does [person] intend to make you feel this way, or is it more of an unintended effect?"

### Phase 3: Motivation Assessment

**Relationship motivation:**
> "If you're honest with yourself, why are you in this relationship?"
> "If all the practical considerations disappeared — finances, children, social pressure — would you still choose this relationship?"
> "What keeps you here on the hard days?"

**Change motivation:**
> "What would need to change for this relationship to feel good again?"
> "How motivated are you to work on this? Where does that motivation come from?"

## Hypothesis Update Rules

### Prior
Start with no assumption about which needs are affected. Assume some degree of distress (they're seeking help).

### Signal → Variable Mapping

| Signal | Variable Update |
|--------|----------------|
| "I have no say" / "They decide everything" / "I can't be myself" | ↑ Autonomy Frustration |
| "I just feel like I'm going through the motions" / lack of initiative | ↑ Low Autonomy Satisfaction |
| "Nothing I do is good enough" / "I can't do anything right" | ↑ Competence Frustration |
| "I don't know what to do" / "I feel stuck" | ↑ Low Competence Satisfaction |
| "I feel alone even when we're together" / "They don't see me" | ↑ Relatedness Frustration |
| "We just don't connect anymore" / "It's flat" | ↑ Low Relatedness Satisfaction |
| "I stay because of the kids/finances/guilt" | External/Introjected Motivation |
| "I love them but I'm not happy" | Identified but frustrated |
| "This is who I want to be with" | Integrated Motivation |
| Reports controlling partner behavior | Partner as Autonomy Thwarting source |
| Reports critical/undermining partner | Partner as Competence Thwarting source |
| Reports emotionally unavailable partner | Partner as Relatedness Thwarting source |
| "I choose to be here" / "I want this" | ↑ Autonomy Satisfaction |
| "I'm good at this" / "I handled that well" | ↑ Competence Satisfaction |
| "They really understand me" / "I feel loved" | ↑ Relatedness Satisfaction |

### Urgency Assessment

| Pattern | Urgency | Rationale |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Active frustration on any need | High | Active thwarting is damaging and tends to escalate |
| Active frustration on multiple needs | Very High | Comprehensive need thwarting = high risk of relationship dissolution or psychological harm |
| Low satisfaction without frustration | Moderate | Relationship is stagnant but not toxic |
| External/introjected motivation only | High | The person is staying for wrong reasons; autonomy being compromised |
| Frustration + low motivation to change | Critical | Person may be at exit point |

### Confidence Calibration
- Autonomy frustration can be confused with attachment anxiety (both involve "I can't be myself"). Cross-reference with attachment assessment.
- Competence frustration can be confused with low self-esteem that predates the relationship. Check if it's relationship-specific.
- Relatedness frustration can look like partner avoidance — distinguish between "they won't connect" (partner issue) and "I can't reach them" (interactional issue).
- Cultural context matters — some cultures emphasize duty/obligation in relationships (external/introjected regulation) as normative.

## Interaction / Decision Implications

### When Autonomy Is Frustrated

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Name the autonomy need explicitly: "It sounds like you don't feel like you have real choice here" | Tell them what to do (this further thwarts autonomy!) |
| Explore what choice would look like for them | Prescribe solutions |
| Help identify specific controlling dynamics | Blame them for "allowing" it |
| Support their right to have preferences and boundaries | Frame autonomy needs as selfish |
| Ask permission before giving advice: "Would it be helpful if I shared a thought?" | Push advice without invitation |

### When Competence Is Frustrated

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Identify and reflect back their strengths and past successes | Add to their sense of inadequacy |
| Help them see the impact of their efforts, even small ones | Frame their difficulties as personal failures |
| Break overwhelming problems into manageable steps | Present complex plans that overwhelm |
| Validate that the situation may genuinely be difficult (not just their inadequacy) | Imply that competence alone should solve it |
| Build efficacy through small wins | Set them up for failure with unrealistic expectations |

### When Relatedness Is Frustrated

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Demonstrate genuine interest and understanding (model what's missing) | Be transactional or clinical |
| Help them articulate what connection looks like for them | Assume you know what connection they need |
| Explore whether the partner is aware of the disconnection | Blame either party for the gap |
| Distinguish between different expressions of care | Impose one version of "connection" |
| Assess whether relatedness needs are being met elsewhere | Assume the romantic partner must meet all relatedness needs |

### MI-Informed Communication Style (apply across all interactions)

The system should embody need-supportive communication:

1. **Ask permission**: "Would it be helpful if I shared an observation?" (supports autonomy)
2. **Offer, don't prescribe**: "One thing some people find helpful is... What do you think?" (supports autonomy)
3. **Reflect and validate**: "It sounds like you're feeling..." (supports relatedness)
4. **Highlight competence**: "You've already shown you can... because you did..." (supports competence)
5. **Explore ambivalence**: "Part of you wants X, and part of you wants Y. Tell me more about both sides." (supports autonomy)
6. **Roll with resistance**: If the user pushes back on a suggestion, explore their perspective rather than insisting (supports autonomy)

**Key principle**: The system's communication style should ITSELF satisfy basic needs. If the system is controlling (prescriptive), undermining (dismissive of user's capacity), or disconnected (clinical/transactional), it is modeling the very dynamics it should be helping to resolve. The medium is the message.
