# Trust Architecture Assessment

## Applicable Contexts

- User says they "don't trust" someone or feel trust has been broken
- User describes betrayal, dishonesty, or feeling let down
- User is deciding whether to give someone another chance
- User describes being hurt by someone's incompetence, selfishness, or dishonesty
- User describes difficulty trusting people in general
- User is trying to rebuild trust after a violation
- User describes feeling suspicious or hypervigilant in a relationship
- User is deciding how much to rely on or confide in someone

## Theoretical Basis

The Integrative Model of Trust (Mayer, Davis & Schoorman, 1995; Schoorman, Mayer & Davis, 2007) identifies three dimensions of trustworthiness that independently contribute to trust:

1. **Ability** (Competence): The skills, expertise, and capacity of the trustee to perform in a specific domain. Domain-specific — you can trust someone's professional competence but not their emotional competence.

2. **Benevolence**: The extent to which the trustee is believed to want to do good for the trustor, beyond self-interest. "Do they care about my wellbeing?" Relationship-specific — develops over time through demonstrated care.

3. **Integrity**: The perception that the trustee adheres to principles the trustor finds acceptable. Consistency between words and actions. Predictability of moral behavior. Most difficult to repair once violated.

**Key distinctions:**

- **Trust ≠ Trustworthiness**: Trust is the willingness to be vulnerable. Trustworthiness is the property of the person being trusted. The ABI model assesses trustworthiness to inform trust decisions.
- **Propensity to Trust**: A stable individual difference — some people trust easily, others don't, independent of the trustee's actual trustworthiness. This is a property of the TRUSTOR, not the relationship.
- **Risk**: Trust is only relevant when there is something at stake. Low-stakes situations don't test trust.

**Trust Violation and Repair** (Kim et al., 2004, 2006; Schweitzer et al., 2006):

The critical insight is that **repair strategy must match violation type**:

| Violation Type | Effective Repair | Ineffective Repair |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Ability (competence failure) | Apology + demonstration of improved competence | Denial (makes it worse) |
| Benevolence (selfish act) | Apology + sustained selfless behavior | Quick apology alone (insufficient) |
| Integrity (moral violation) | Denial may work initially; if proven, sustained behavioral change over time | Quick apology (paradoxically confirms the violation was intentional) |

**Counterintuitive finding**: For integrity violations, apologizing can actually DECREASE trust, because an apology implies intentionality. If someone says "I'm sorry I lied," they're confirming they chose to lie. For competence violations, apology works well because it implies the failure was unintentional.

**Trust asymmetry**: Trust is built slowly (through many small positive interactions) and destroyed quickly (through a single violation). The severity and type of violation determine recovery trajectory.

## Key Variables / Dimensions

| Variable | Range | What It Captures |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| Ability-Trust | 0.0 – 1.0 | Belief in the other's competence in relevant domains |
| Benevolence-Trust | 0.0 – 1.0 | Belief that the other genuinely cares about the trustor's wellbeing |
| Integrity-Trust | 0.0 – 1.0 | Belief that the other adheres to acceptable principles |
| Propensity to Trust | 0.0 – 1.0 | Trustor's general tendency to trust others (cross-relationship) |
| Violation Type | {none, ability, benevolence, integrity, multiple} | What type of trust was violated |
| Violation Severity | 0.0 – 1.0 | How severe the violation was |
| Repair Stage | {not needed, not attempted, early, ongoing, partial, complete} | Where in the repair process |
| Vulnerability Willingness | 0.0 – 1.0 | Current willingness to be vulnerable with this person |

**Track separately for each direction in the relationship (A trusts B vs. B trusts A).**

## Required Evidence

### Minimum evidence before hypothesis formation:
- At least 1 specific trust-relevant episode described in detail
- Understanding of what "trust" means to THIS user (which dimension is salient)
- At least 1 indicator of whether this is relationship-specific or a general pattern

### Evidence channels:
1. **Violation narratives** — What exactly happened, described behaviorally
2. **Attribution patterns** — How the user explains WHY it happened (intent vs. circumstance)
3. **Cross-relationship trust patterns** — Trust issues with many people = propensity issue
4. **Recovery behavior** — What the violator has done since the violation
5. **Vulnerability behavior** — What the user is/isn't willing to risk with this person now
6. **Emotional tone** — Anger (benevolence/integrity violation) vs. disappointment (ability) vs. disgust (integrity)

### Critical assessment principle:
Users often present undifferentiated "I don't trust them." The first task is ALWAYS to disambiguate which dimension(s) of trust are affected. The intervention is completely different depending on the answer. "They can't be trusted" can mean "they're incompetent" (ability), "they don't care about me" (benevolence), or "they're dishonest" (integrity) — and these require very different responses.

## Probing Questions

### Phase 1: Trust Disambiguation (most critical phase)

**Identifying the dimension:**
> "When you say you don't trust [person], can you help me understand what that means specifically? Is it more that you don't trust their ability to follow through, that you're not sure they have your best interests at heart, or that you question their honesty and principles?"
> "What specifically did [person] do that damaged your trust?"
> "If [person] promised to do X, what would worry you most — that they couldn't do it, that they wouldn't prioritize it, or that they might not be honest about it?"

**Attribution probing:**
> "Why do you think [person] did that? Do you think it was intentional, or more of a failure to follow through?"
> "Before this happened, had you seen any warning signs?"

**Severity assessment:**
> "On a scale of 'disappointed' to 'devastated,' where does this fall for you?"
> "Has this changed how you see [person] fundamentally, or is it more of a specific incident?"

### Phase 2: Propensity Assessment (cross-relationship)

**General trust pattern:**
> "Is this something that's specific to [person], or do you find it hard to trust people in general?"
> "In your other close relationships, how easily do you trust?"
> "Has there been a time in the past when someone broke your trust in a similar way?"

**Trust history:**
> "Growing up, were the important people in your life generally reliable and honest?"
> "Have you been through situations before where trust was broken and then rebuilt? What was that like?"

### Phase 3: Repair Assessment (if violation has occurred)

**Current repair status:**
> "Has [person] acknowledged what happened? What did they say or do?"
> "What would [person] need to do for you to start trusting them again? Is that realistic?"
> "How would you know if [person] had genuinely changed — what would you need to see?"

**Readiness assessment:**
> "Do you want to rebuild trust with [person], or are you trying to decide whether it's worth it?"
> "What would it cost you to stay in this relationship without trusting them?"

## Hypothesis Update Rules

### Prior
Start with no assumption about which trust dimension is affected. Assume moderate propensity to trust until cross-relationship evidence suggests otherwise.

### Signal → Variable Mapping

| Signal | Variable Update |
|--------|----------------|
| "They can't do it" / "They're incompetent" / "They always mess up" | ↓ Ability-Trust |
| "They don't care about me" / "They're selfish" / "They only think of themselves" | ↓ Benevolence-Trust |
| "They lied" / "They're dishonest" / "They have no principles" | ↓ Integrity-Trust |
| Trust issues across multiple relationships | Low Propensity to Trust |
| "I generally trust people until proven otherwise" | High Propensity to Trust |
| Anger/hurt about the violation | Benevolence or Integrity violation |
| Disappointment/frustration about the violation | Ability violation |
| Disgust/moral outrage about the violation | Integrity violation |
| "I was naive" / self-blame after violation | Low Propensity updating downward |
| "They've been trying" / describes repair behavior | Repair in progress |
| "Nothing they do will change what they did" | Repair blocked (likely integrity violation) |
| Still willing to depend on person in some domains | Domain-specific trust damage (ability) |
| Complete withdrawal from the relationship | Severe violation (likely integrity or benevolence) |

### Repair Prognosis Assessment

| Violation Type | Repair Difficulty | Key Repair Mechanism |
|---------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Ability | Moderate | Demonstration of improved competence + apology |
| Benevolence | High | Sustained selfless behavior over time + genuine apology |
| Integrity | Very High | Long-term behavioral consistency; apology may backfire |
| Multiple dimensions | Extremely High | Address each dimension separately; consider professional help |

### Confidence Calibration
- The user's account of the violation is filtered through their emotional state and attachment patterns
- Anxiously attached individuals may perceive benevolence violations where none exist
- Avoidantly attached individuals may minimize genuine violations
- Cross-reference trust narratives with attachment assessment for calibration
- A single dramatic violation may overshadow months of trustworthy behavior — assess proportionality

## Interaction / Decision Implications

### When Ability-Trust Is Low

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Validate the frustration of relying on someone who can't deliver | Frame it as a personal failing of the trustee |
| Help identify specific domains where competence is lacking | Generalize incompetence across all domains |
| Explore whether expectations are realistic for this person | Pressure the user to "just trust them" |
| Discuss whether support/training could address the gap | Ignore the legitimate practical impact |
| Help set up structures that reduce dependence on this dimension | Dismiss ability concerns as "petty" |

### When Benevolence-Trust Is Low

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Validate the pain of feeling uncared for | Immediately defend the other person's intentions |
| Explore specific behaviors that signal lack of care | Accept "they don't care" at face value without exploration |
| Distinguish between different love languages / care expressions | Minimize the user's need to feel cared for |
| Help identify what demonstrations of care would be meaningful | Push forgiveness before processing |
| Assess whether the other person is aware of the impact | Assume malice without evidence |

### When Integrity-Trust Is Low

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Take it seriously — integrity violations are the most damaging | Minimize dishonesty or principle violations |
| Explore whether the violation represents a pattern or isolated event | Encourage quick forgiveness |
| Be honest about the difficulty of repairing integrity trust | Suggest that apology alone will fix it |
| Help assess whether behavioral change is genuine or performative | Push "everyone makes mistakes" prematurely |
| Discuss what sustained behavioral evidence would look like | Ignore the user's moral framework |

### When Propensity to Trust Is Low

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Validate that distrust often has protective origins | Pathologize low trust as a "problem" |
| Explore the history that shaped their trust stance | Push them to trust more quickly |
| Help distinguish earned distrust from generalized vigilance | Dismiss their pattern-recognition as paranoia |
| Support gradual, evidence-based trust building | Demand vulnerability before safety is established |
| Connect to attachment patterns (avoidant attachment often manifests as low propensity) | Ignore the adaptive function of their caution |

### Repair Coaching (violation-type specific)

**For Ability Violations:**
- Encourage the violator to acknowledge the failure directly
- Focus on concrete steps to improve competence
- Suggest structured check-ins to rebuild confidence
- Frame as: "This is about capability, not character"

**For Benevolence Violations:**
- The violator needs to demonstrate care through ACTIONS, not words
- Sustained, consistent, small acts of prioritizing the trustor
- Help the trustor articulate what demonstrations of care they need
- Frame as: "This is about showing care in ways that land"

**For Integrity Violations:**
- Be cautious with apology — it can confirm intentionality
- Focus on sustained behavioral consistency over time
- The trustor needs to see the violator resist temptation, not just apologize
- Frame as: "Words matter less than patterns here. What matters is what they do next, consistently"
- Be honest: integrity repair is the hardest and sometimes not possible

**Key principle**: Never push forgiveness. Trust is rebuilt through evidence, not declarations. The trustor sets the pace. The system's role is to help the user make clear-eyed assessments of trustworthiness and make informed decisions about vulnerability.
