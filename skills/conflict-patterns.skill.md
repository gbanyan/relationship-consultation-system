# Conflict Pattern Recognition

## Applicable Contexts

- User describes an argument, disagreement, or recurring conflict with someone
- User asks how to bring up a difficult topic or have a hard conversation
- User describes feeling unheard, criticized, or dismissed during conflict
- User describes a partner who shuts down, gets defensive, or escalates
- User describes a relationship where "we can't even talk anymore"
- User describes contemptuous or dismissive behavior (eye-rolling, sarcasm, name-calling)
- User reports feeling flooded or overwhelmed during arguments

## Theoretical Basis

John Gottman's research (Gottman, 1994, 1999; Gottman & Silver, 1999) is the most empirically validated framework for predicting relationship outcomes from observable communication patterns. Based on longitudinal studies of thousands of couples, the framework identifies specific destructive patterns (the "Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse") and protective factors (repair attempts, positive sentiment override) that predict relationship stability with over 90% accuracy.

**The Four Horsemen** (in order of severity):

1. **Criticism**: Attacking character rather than addressing specific behavior. "You never..." / "You always..." / "What's wrong with you?" Distinguished from *complaint* (specific, behavioral, situational).

2. **Contempt**: Superiority, disgust, mockery, eye-rolling, sarcasm directed at the person. The single strongest predictor of relationship dissolution. Fueled by long-simmering negative thoughts (Negative Sentiment Override). Includes: sarcasm, cynicism, name-calling, eye-rolling, sneering, mockery, hostile humor.

3. **Defensiveness**: Counter-attacking or playing the victim in response to perceived criticism. Cross-complaining, "yes-but" responses, righteous indignation. Blocks the partner's complaint from being heard.

4. **Stonewalling**: Emotional withdrawal, shutting down, disengaging. Usually occurs when the listener becomes physiologically flooded (heart rate >100 BPM). More common in men (85% of stonewallers in Gottman's research). NOT the same as healthy self-soothing breaks.

**The Cascade Model**: Criticism → Contempt → Defensiveness → Stonewalling → Emotional Disengagement → Relationship Dissolution. Earlier intervention = better prognosis.

**Repair Attempts**: Any statement or action during conflict that prevents negativity from escalating. The success or failure of repair attempts is the most important predictor — not their frequency. Repair works when there's a foundation of friendship (positive sentiment override).

**The 5:1 Ratio**: Stable relationships maintain roughly 5 positive interactions for every 1 negative interaction during conflict discussions. Unstable relationships drop below 1:1.

**Harsh vs. Soft Startup**: How a conversation begins predicts how it ends with 96% accuracy. Harsh startup (criticism, contempt, "you" statements) → negative outcome. Soft startup (I-statements, specific complaint, appreciation) → productive outcome.

**Flooding**: Physiological overwhelm during conflict. When flooded, the person cannot process information, take perspective, or engage constructively. Signs: elevated heart rate, muscle tension, desire to flee or fight. Requires a minimum 20-minute break for physiological recovery.

## Key Variables / Dimensions

| Variable | Range / Type | What It Captures |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Criticism Frequency | 0.0 – 1.0 | How often character attacks replace specific complaints |
| Contempt Presence | 0.0 – 1.0 | Degree of superiority/disgust expressed toward partner |
| Defensiveness Level | 0.0 – 1.0 | Tendency to counter-attack or victim-play vs. accept influence |
| Stonewalling Frequency | 0.0 – 1.0 | Frequency of emotional withdrawal during conflict |
| Cascade Stage | {none, early, middle, late} | How far along the dissolution cascade |
| Repair Attempt Efficacy | 0.0 – 1.0 | Whether repair attempts are made AND accepted |
| Positive:Negative Ratio | numeric ratio | Estimated ratio of positive to negative interactions |
| Startup Pattern | {soft, mixed, harsh} | How conflicts are typically initiated |
| Flooding Vulnerability | 0.0 – 1.0 | Susceptibility to physiological overwhelm |
| Sentiment Override | {positive, neutral, negative} | Default interpretation lens for partner's behavior |

**Track for BOTH the user AND described partner where possible.**

## Required Evidence

### Minimum evidence before hypothesis formation:
- At least 1 detailed conflict narrative (step-by-step account of an argument)
- At least 1 description of how conflicts typically end (repair or rupture)
- At least 1 indicator of the overall relationship climate (positive:negative balance)

### Evidence channels (ordered by diagnostic power):
1. **Described dialogue** — Actual words used during conflict (or close paraphrases)
2. **Behavioral sequences** — What happened after what (escalation patterns)
3. **Physiological signals** — Reports of feeling flooded, shutting down, heart racing
4. **Meta-conflict patterns** — Do they fight about the same things? Can they laugh during arguments?
5. **Repair descriptions** — What happens when someone tries to de-escalate
6. **Overall climate descriptions** — Fondness, admiration, friendship vs. resentment

### Critical assessment principle:
People often conflate complaint and criticism. "They always criticize me" may describe legitimate complaints that the user perceives as attacks due to their own defensiveness. Cross-reference the described words with the Horseman definitions. The user's INTERPRETATION of their partner's behavior is itself data — about the user's sentiment override.

## Probing Questions

### Phase 1: Conflict Mapping (identify what's happening)

**Opening narrative:**
> "Tell me about a recent argument or disagreement with [person]. What triggered it, and how did it unfold?"
> "When you think about how you and [person] argue, is there a pattern you notice?"

**Horseman detection:**
> "When [person] brings up a problem, how do they typically phrase it? Can you remember specific words?"
> "When you're upset with [person], how do you usually bring it up?"
> "Has there ever been a moment during an argument where one of you just... shut down? What did that look like?"
> "Does either of you ever use sarcasm or eye-rolling during disagreements?"

**Startup pattern:**
> "Think about the last time one of you brought up something that bothered them. How did that conversation start — the very first thing that was said?"

### Phase 2: Repair and Recovery (identify what resources exist)

**Repair attempts:**
> "When an argument is escalating, does either of you ever try to lighten the mood, take a break, or change direction? What happens when they do?"
> "After an argument, how do you two reconnect? How long does it take?"

**Flooding detection:**
> "During arguments, do you ever feel like you just can't think straight, or like your body is in fight-or-flight mode? What do you do when that happens?"
> "Does [person] ever seem to just completely check out during a difficult conversation?"

**Positive foundation:**
> "Outside of conflicts, how would you describe the friendship between you and [person]?"
> "Can you tell me about something [person] did recently that you genuinely appreciated?"
> "When things are good between you, what does that look like?"

### Phase 3: Pattern Assessment (once Horsemen identified)

**Cascade assessment:**
> "How long has this pattern been going on? Has it gotten worse over time?"
> "Do you still feel like working on the relationship is worth it?"
> "Do you find yourself rewriting the history of your relationship — seeing the past more negatively than you used to?"

**Gridlock vs. solvable:**
> "This issue you keep arguing about — do you feel like it represents something deeper than the surface topic?"
> "Is there a dream or value underneath your position that feels non-negotiable?"

## Hypothesis Update Rules

### Prior
Start with no assumptions about Horseman presence. Assume baseline positive sentiment until evidence suggests otherwise.

### Signal → Variable Mapping

| Signal | Variable Update |
|--------|----------------|
| "You always..." / "You never..." / character attacks | ↑ Criticism |
| Sarcasm, mockery, eye-rolling, name-calling, sneering | ↑ Contempt |
| "Yes, but..." / counter-complaining / "It's not my fault" | ↑ Defensiveness |
| Goes silent, walks away, "checks out," glazed look | ↑ Stonewalling |
| Reports heart racing, inability to think, wanting to flee | ↑ Flooding Vulnerability |
| Humor during conflict, reaching out after, saying "I'm sorry" | ↑ Repair Efficacy |
| Partner accepts humor/apology, de-escalates | ↑↑ Repair Efficacy |
| Partner rejects repair, re-escalates after attempt | ↓↓ Repair Efficacy |
| Describes ongoing fondness, admiration, friendship | ↑ Positive Sentiment |
| "I've given up" / "Nothing will change" / rewritten history | Late Cascade Stage |
| Describes specific behavior + feelings, not character | Soft Startup indicator |
| Can discuss disagreement without visible distress | Healthy conflict indicator |

### Cascade Stage Assessment

| Evidence Pattern | Stage |
|-----------------|-------|
| Occasional criticism, repair works, positive foundation strong | None/Early |
| Regular criticism + defensiveness cycle, contempt emerging | Early |
| Contempt present, repair failing, negative sentiment override | Middle |
| Stonewalling regular, emotional disengagement, history rewriting | Late |

### Confidence Calibration
- The user's version is always partial — they may not see their own Horsemen
- Ask about BOTH partners' behavior, not just the described partner
- A single conflict narrative may not be representative — look for patterns
- Cultural and gender norms affect conflict expression — stonewalling may look different across cultures
- Distinguish between healthy time-outs (with stated return) and stonewalling (unilateral withdrawal)

## Interaction / Decision Implications

### When Criticism Is Dominant

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Teach the difference between complaint and criticism | Label the user as "critical" |
| Model soft startup: "I feel X when Y happens. I need Z" | Dismiss the underlying complaint |
| Validate the frustration while redirecting the expression | Imply their partner is blameless |
| Help identify the specific behavior (not character) that bothers them | Rush past emotional validation |

### When Contempt Is Detected

**This is the most urgent intervention target.** Contempt is the strongest predictor of dissolution and the most corrosive to relationship health.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Name it clearly but without shaming: "That sounds like it carries real disgust" | Ignore it or minimize it |
| Explore the long-simmering resentments fueling it | Lecture about how contempt is "bad" |
| Build the culture of appreciation as an antidote | Assume quick fixes will work |
| Assess whether the relationship has enough positive foundation to rebuild | Blindly encourage staying together |

### When Defensiveness Is the Pattern

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Help them see their partner's complaint underneath the criticism | Take sides |
| Practice accepting influence: "What's the kernel of truth in what they're saying?" | Frame accepting influence as weakness |
| Model non-defensive responses | Push too hard (triggers more defensiveness) |

### When Stonewalling Is Present

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Normalize flooding as physiological, not emotional weakness | Interpret stonewalling as not caring |
| Teach the 20-minute physiological recovery rule | Encourage "pushing through" when flooded |
| Help develop a structured time-out protocol | Frame breaks as avoidance |
| Address the underlying flooding vulnerability | Demand immediate engagement |

### When Repair Attempts Are Failing

This is the most critical diagnostic signal. When repair fails consistently:
- The positive sentiment reservoir is depleted
- Rebuild friendship FIRST (Gottman's Sound Relationship House foundation)
- Do not coach conflict skills until the friendship is stronger
- Focus on small positive interactions, turning toward bids, building fondness and admiration

### Cascade-Specific Guidance

| Stage | Priority |
|-------|----------|
| None/Early | Teach soft startup, coach specific complaints vs. criticism |
| Early-Middle | Address contempt urgently, rebuild appreciation, strengthen friendship |
| Middle | Repair attempt coaching, flooding management, assess for gridlock |
| Late | Honest assessment of whether recovery is possible, recommend professional help |

**Key principle**: The order of intervention matters. You cannot fix conflict skills without a friendship foundation, and you cannot rebuild friendship while contempt is active. Work backwards from the most severe Horseman present.
