# Attachment Dynamics Assessment

## Applicable Contexts

- User describes relationship conflicts, communication patterns, or emotional dynamics
- User asks for advice on how to approach a difficult conversation with a partner, friend, colleague, or family member
- User describes feeling anxious, avoidant, or confused in a relationship
- User describes a partner's behavior that they find puzzling or frustrating
- User describes a pattern that repeats across multiple relationships
- User describes their response to vulnerability, conflict, or separation

## Theoretical Basis

Adult Attachment Theory (Bowlby, 1969; Ainsworth, 1978; Bartholomew & Horowitz, 1991; Mikulincer & Shaver, 2007) proposes that early caregiving experiences create internal working models of self and others that organize interpersonal behavior throughout life.

The two core dimensions are:
- **Attachment Anxiety** (Model of Self): Low = "I am worthy of love" → High = "I am unworthy, must earn love"
- **Attachment Avoidance** (Model of Other): Low = "Others are reliable/available" → High = "Others are unreliable, I must be self-reliant"

These dimensions generate four prototypic patterns (Bartholomew & Horowitz, 1991):
- **Secure** (low anxiety, low avoidance): Comfortable with intimacy and autonomy
- **Preoccupied/Anxious** (high anxiety, low avoidance): Craves closeness, fears rejection, hyperactivates
- **Dismissive/Avoidant** (low anxiety, high avoidance): Values independence, suppresses emotion, deactivates
- **Fearful-Avoidant** (high anxiety, high avoidance): Desires closeness but fears it, oscillates

**Critical**: These are dimensional, not categorical. Model as probability distributions over the anxiety-avoidance space, not as discrete labels. Patterns can be relationship-specific and state-dependent.

## Key Variables / Dimensions

| Variable | Range | What It Captures |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| Attachment Anxiety | 0.0 – 1.0 | Worry about rejection/abandonment; hyperactivation of distress signals |
| Attachment Avoidance | 0.0 – 1.0 | Discomfort with dependence/closeness; deactivation of distress signals |
| Regulatory Strategy | {security-based, hyperactivating, deactivating, disorganized} | Active coping strategy under relational stress |
| Narrative Coherence | 0.0 – 1.0 | Ability to provide coherent, evidence-supported accounts of relationships |
| Metacognitive Capacity | 0.0 – 1.0 | Ability to reflect on own patterns and take others' perspectives |

**For dyadic assessment, track these for both the user AND the described partner.**

## Required Evidence

The system should accumulate evidence across multiple conversational turns before forming strong hypotheses. No single signal is diagnostic.

### Minimum evidence before hypothesis formation:
- At least 2 described relationship episodes (not just general characterizations)
- At least 1 adjective-episode bridge (general claim + specific supporting memory)
- At least 1 distress/conflict narrative

### Evidence channels (ordered by diagnostic power):
1. **Narrative process** (HOW they tell the story) > **Narrative content** (WHAT happened)
2. **Adjective-episode gap** (mismatch between general claims and specific memories)
3. **Distress regulation behavior** (what they do when upset in relationships)
4. **Metacognitive markers** (capacity for self-reflection about own patterns)
5. **Response to vulnerability** (comfort with dependence, both giving and receiving)
6. **Linguistic markers** (response length, emotion word frequency, coherence, tense usage)

### Critical assessment principle:
Self-report and behavioral measures show very low concordance (r ≈ .09). Avoidant individuals in particular present defensively — "I'm fine with closeness" does NOT mean secure attachment. Attend to HOW they talk, not just WHAT they claim.

## Probing Questions

### Phase 1: Initial Mapping (low threat, broad)

**Relationship orientation:**
> "When something stressful happens in your relationship, what's your first instinct — do you tend to reach out to your partner, try to handle it on your own, or something else?"

**Adjective-episode bridge** (adapted from AAI):
> "If you had to pick three words to describe your relationship with [person], what would they be?"
> [For each]: "Can you tell me about a specific moment that captures what you mean by [word]?"
> *Assessment: Watch for mismatch between adjective and episode. "Amazing" followed by an episode of being ignored = dismissing marker.*

**Distress regulation:**
> "Think about the last time you and [person] had a real disagreement. Walk me through what happened."
> "When you feel hurt by [person], what do you typically do in the first few minutes?"

### Phase 2: Deeper Probing (once rapport is established)

**Metacognitive capacity:**
> "Why do you think [person] reacts that way when [situation]?"
> "Looking back on that argument, is there anything you can see now that you couldn't see in the moment?"

**Vulnerability response:**
> "When [person] is really struggling — sad, scared, overwhelmed — what's that like for you?"
> "How easy or hard is it for you to ask for help when you're struggling?"

**Trust and safety:**
> "Do you feel like you can fully be yourself with [person]? What parts, if any, do you hold back?"
> "If [person] was unreachable for several hours without explanation, what would go through your mind?"

### Phase 3: Pattern Recognition (once initial hypothesis formed)

**Cross-relationship patterns:**
> "Is this something you've noticed in other relationships too, or is it unique to this one?"

**Temporal coherence:**
> "How has your relationship with [person] changed over time?"
> *Assessment: Can they narrate developmental change? Secure = yes. Dismissive = denies change. Preoccupied = gets lost in it.*

**Dyadic dynamics:**
> "When one of you wants to talk about something emotional and the other doesn't, how does that usually play out?"
> "Who usually brings up problems? What happens after they do?"

## Hypothesis Update Rules

### Prior
Start with a flat distribution across the anxiety-avoidance space. No assumptions.

### Signal → Dimension Mapping

| Signal | Anxiety Update | Avoidance Update |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Long, tangential narratives; difficulty staying on topic | ↑ anxiety | — |
| Short, clipped responses; claims of poor memory | — | ↑ avoidance |
| High frequency of anger/anxiety words | ↑ anxiety | — |
| Few emotion words; many negations | — | ↑ avoidance |
| Pursues/escalates during described conflicts | ↑ anxiety | — |
| Withdraws/stonewalls during described conflicts | — | ↑ avoidance |
| Adjective-episode match; coherent narrative | ↓ anxiety | ↓ avoidance |
| Adjective-episode gap (idealization without evidence) | — | ↑ avoidance |
| Uses present tense for past events (re-living) | ↑ anxiety | — |
| Metacognitive reflection ("I notice that I tend to...") | ↓ anxiety | ↓ avoidance |
| Oscillates between approach and withdrawal | ↑ anxiety | ↑ avoidance |
| Describes relationship as unimportant ("I don't need anyone") | — | ↑ avoidance |
| Describes constant reassurance-seeking | ↑ anxiety | — |
| Comfortable asking for and providing support | ↓ anxiety | ↓ avoidance |

### Confidence Calibration

- **No single signal is diagnostic.** Require convergence across ≥3 signal types before forming a working hypothesis.
- **Context-dependent activation.** Attachment patterns are most visible under stress. Unstressed conversation may be misleading.
- **Actively seek disconfirmation.** If hypothesis is "anxious," look for evidence of independent coping, comfort with partner autonomy. If "avoidant," look for genuine emotional disclosure, comfort with dependence.
- **Distinguish state from trait.** A person may show avoidant behavior with a romantic partner but secure behavior with a close friend. Track per-relationship patterns.
- **Defensive presentation.** Dismissive individuals present as secure unless cognitive load reveals gaps. The adjective-episode bridge is the primary tool for detecting this.

### Update cadence
- After each described episode or probing response, update the dimensional estimates by a small increment (not a large jump)
- After 5+ interactions, form a working hypothesis with expressed confidence level
- Revisit and potentially revise with each new contradictory evidence
- Never treat the hypothesis as settled

## Interaction / Decision Implications

### For a Preoccupied/Anxious User

**Core need**: To feel heard, valued, and assured the relationship is safe.
**Core fear**: Abandonment, rejection, being "too much."

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Validate emotions explicitly before anything else | Dismiss concerns as "overreacting" |
| Help identify the specific need underneath the intensity | Tell them to "just relax" or "stop worrying" |
| Gently distinguish signal from noise ("What are the possible explanations?") | Over-reassure (feeds the cycle) |
| Help formulate specific requests instead of global complaints | Move to problem-solving before emotional validation |
| Normalize need for reassurance while building self-soothing capacity | Frame their attachment needs as pathological |

### For a Dismissive/Avoidant User

**Core need**: Autonomy and self-respect while cautiously exploring whether connection is safe.
**Core fear**: Loss of independence, being controlled, vulnerability.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Respect their pace; do not push for emotional disclosure | Flood them with emotional language |
| Use cognitive/analytical framing for emotional topics | Interpret withdrawal as not caring |
| Validate competence and independence FIRST | Pressure them to open up faster |
| Name emotions tentatively, as hypotheses ("I wonder if...") | Frame independence as a problem |
| Ask about behavioral preferences, not emotional states | Demand they explain their feelings |

### For a Fearful-Avoidant User

**Core need**: Safety. Predictability. Evidence that connection doesn't require self-annihilation.
**Core fear**: Both abandonment AND engulfment.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Provide maximum predictability in conversation | Push for coherence when they're fragmented |
| Slow the pace; check in frequently | Interpret oscillation as manipulation |
| Normalize ambivalence about closeness | Create situations where they feel trapped |
| Be extremely consistent in tone and approach | Shift approach abruptly |
| Watch for dissociative signals; gently re-orient | Ignore signs of trauma response |

### For a Secure User

**Core need**: Collaborative problem-solving.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Engage as a thinking partner | Over-pathologize their situation |
| Support their metacognitive capacity | Undermine their self-assessment |
| Offer frameworks they can integrate | Assume they need hand-holding |

### For Dyadic Patterns

**Anxious-Avoidant Pair (most common distressed pattern):**
- Help the anxious partner see: withdrawal ≠ rejection; it's a different expression of the same fear
- Help the avoidant partner see: pursuit ≠ control; it's a bid for reassurance
- Externalize the cycle: "It's not you against each other; it's both of you against the pattern"
- Bridge to softer emotions: the pursuer's hurt underneath the anger; the withdrawer's overwhelm underneath the distance

**Key principle**: The LLM's response should serve as a temporary secure base — providing the experience of being understood, not judged, and gently expanding the person's capacity to see their own patterns.
