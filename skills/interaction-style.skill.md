# Interaction Style Mapping

## Applicable Contexts

- User describes interpersonal dynamics involving power, control, warmth, or coldness
- User describes someone as "dominating," "passive," "cold," "clingy," "aggressive," or "pushover"
- User notices they or others behave the same way regardless of context
- User describes difficulty with a specific person's interpersonal style
- User describes being "pulled into" certain behaviors by another person
- User asks why they always end up in similar relational dynamics
- System needs to map structural interaction patterns for a dyad

## Theoretical Basis

The Interpersonal Circumplex (IPC; Leary, 1957; Wiggins, 1979; Kiesler, 1983) organizes interpersonal behavior along two fundamental, orthogonal dimensions:

1. **Agency** (Dominance--Submission axis, vertical): The degree to which a person exerts control, influence, and assertion versus deferring, yielding, and accommodating. High agency = leading, directing, taking charge. Low agency = following, accommodating, deferring.

2. **Communion** (Warmth--Hostility axis, horizontal): The degree to which a person connects warmly, affiliates, and nurtures versus distances, opposes, and acts coldly. High communion = warm, friendly, nurturing. Low communion = cold, hostile, detached.

These two dimensions create a circular (circumplex) structure with **eight octants**:

| Octant | Agency | Communion | Description | Behavioral Examples |
|--------|--------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| PA (Assured-Dominant) | High + | Neutral | Confident, assertive, leading | Takes charge, makes decisions, directs conversations |
| BC (Arrogant-Calculating) | High + | Low - | Competitive, strategic, self-serving | Manipulates, one-ups, prioritizes own agenda |
| DE (Cold-Hearted) | Neutral | Low - | Distant, hostile, dismissive | Ignores feelings, withholds, criticizes coldly |
| FG (Aloof-Introverted) | Low - | Low - | Withdrawn, isolated, detached | Avoids interaction, stays on periphery, doesn't engage |
| HI (Unassured-Submissive) | Low - | Neutral | Passive, yielding, uncertain | Defers all decisions, can't say no, lacks initiative |
| JK (Unassuming-Ingenuous) | Low - | High + | Modest, agreeable, self-effacing | Goes along with others, avoids spotlight, accommodates |
| LM (Warm-Agreeable) | Neutral | High + | Friendly, caring, supportive | Nurtures, shows interest, affirms, helps |
| NO (Gregarious-Extraverted) | High + | High + | Sociable, enthusiastic, expressive | Energizes groups, initiates contact, shares openly |

**Complementarity Principle** (Kiesler, 1983; Carson, 1969):

Interpersonal behaviors tend to "pull" predictable responses from others:

- **On the Agency axis: Complementarity is OPPOSITE.** Dominance pulls submission, and submission pulls dominance. When one person leads, the other tends to follow (and vice versa).
- **On the Communion axis: Complementarity is RECIPROCAL.** Warmth pulls warmth, and hostility pulls hostility. Friendly behavior invites friendliness; cold behavior invites coldness.

This creates predictable "dances" -- an assertive person will tend to draw out deference in others, and a warm person will tend to draw out warmth.

**Rigidity** (vector length):

The LENGTH of the vector from the origin to a person's typical position indicates rigidity -- how consistently they use the same interpersonal strategy regardless of context. Moderate vector length = adaptive flexibility (can adjust style to situation). Long vector = rigidity (uses same style everywhere, even when it's counterproductive).

Key insight: **Rigidity is more diagnostic than position.** A person who is warm-dominant in all situations (with their boss, their child, their partner, strangers) is showing rigidity that may indicate a characterological pattern. A person who shifts between styles depending on context is showing healthy flexibility.

**Interpersonal Problems:**

Interpersonal problems tend to cluster around the circumplex:
- Too dominant -> controlling, autocratic
- Too submissive -> can't stand up for self, doormat
- Too warm -> overly accommodating, loses self in others
- Too cold -> alienated, hostile, lonely
- Rigidity in any octant -> problematic when the situation requires a different style

## Key Variables / Dimensions

| Variable | Range | What It Captures |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| Agency | -1.0 (submissive) to +1.0 (dominant) | Degree of interpersonal control and assertion |
| Communion | -1.0 (hostile) to +1.0 (warm) | Degree of interpersonal warmth and connection |
| Rigidity | 0.0 (flexible) to 1.0 (rigid) | Consistency of style across contexts |
| Dominant Octant | {PA, BC, DE, FG, HI, JK, LM, NO} | Primary interpersonal style |
| Contextual Variation | {low, moderate, high} | How much style shifts across relationships/contexts |

**Track for BOTH the user AND described partner. Track the DYADIC PATTERN (complementarity or anti-complementarity).**

## Required Evidence

### Minimum evidence before hypothesis formation:
- At least 2 described interactions with behavioral detail (not just emotional reactions)
- At least 1 description of a conflict or disagreement (reveals agency dynamics)
- Ideally, descriptions of the same person in different contexts (to assess rigidity)

### Evidence channels:
1. **Described behavior in conflict** -- Who escalates? Who defers? Who withdraws? Who pursues?
2. **Decision-making patterns** -- Who makes decisions? Is input sought? How are disagreements resolved?
3. **Emotional expression** -- Warm or cold? Affectionate or distant? Responsive or dismissive?
4. **Cross-context behavior** -- Same style with everyone (rigid) or adapts to context (flexible)?
5. **Described "pulls"** -- "They make me feel like I have to..." = complementarity in action
6. **Self-description vs. behavioral evidence** -- "I'm very assertive" but describes always deferring = mismatch

### Critical assessment principle:
People often describe interpersonal style in evaluative terms ("they're controlling," "they're a pushover") that carry moral weight. The circumplex is descriptive, not evaluative. "Dominant" is not inherently bad; "submissive" is not inherently good (or vice versa). Translate evaluative descriptions into circumplex coordinates without the moral loading. "Controlling" = high agency, potentially low communion. "Pushover" = low agency, potentially high communion.

## Probing Questions

### Phase 1: Basic Style Mapping

**Agency assessment:**
> "When you and [person] disagree about something, who usually makes the final call?"
> "In group situations, does [person] tend to take charge, go along with others, or something in between?"
> "How do you typically handle it when someone asks you to do something you don't want to do?"

**Communion assessment:**
> "How does [person] show that they care about people? Or do they tend to keep more of a distance?"
> "When someone around [person] is having a hard time, how do they typically respond?"
> "Would you describe your relationship as warm and affectionate, or more practical and businesslike?"

**Octant identification:**
> "If I asked the people who know [person] best to describe their personality in interactions, what would they say?"
> "What's [person] like at their best? And at their worst?"

### Phase 2: Rigidity Assessment

**Cross-context probing:**
> "Is [person] like this with everyone, or mainly with you?"
> "How does [person] act at work vs. at home? With friends vs. with family?"
> "Are you like this in all your relationships, or is it specific to this one?"

**Flexibility testing:**
> "Can [person] shift gears -- like being assertive when needed but also stepping back?"
> "Have you ever seen [person] be really different from their usual self? What was that like?"

### Phase 3: Complementarity and Dyadic Dynamics

**Pull dynamics:**
> "When [person] acts [dominant/submissive/warm/cold], what do you find yourself doing in response?"
> "Do you feel like [person] brings out a certain side of you that you don't show with others?"
> "Is there a 'dance' between you -- like you each have roles you fall into?"

**Pattern disruption:**
> "What happens when you try to do something different -- like being more assertive when you usually defer, or stepping back when you usually take charge?"
> "Has the dynamic between you and [person] always been this way, or has it shifted?"

## Hypothesis Update Rules

### Prior
Start with no assumption about position. Assume moderate flexibility (average rigidity) until cross-context evidence is available.

### Signal -> Variable Mapping

| Signal | Agency Update | Communion Update |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| Makes decisions for others, takes charge, directs | Agency up | -- |
| Defers decisions, follows others' lead, accommodates | Agency down | -- |
| Expresses warmth, shows care, asks about feelings | -- | Communion up |
| Dismissive, sarcastic, cold, ignores emotional bids | -- | Communion down |
| Competitive, strategic, self-promoting | Agency up | Communion down |
| Self-effacing, agreeable, puts others first | Agency down | Communion up |
| Withdrawn, avoids interaction, isolates | Agency down | Communion down |
| Socially energetic, expressive, initiates contact | Agency up | Communion up |
| Same behavior across all described contexts | Rigidity up | |
| Different style in different relationships | Rigidity down | |
| "They make me feel like I have to [submit/lead]" | Complementarity evidence | |
| "They bring out the worst in me" | Anti-complementary or rigid pattern | |

### Dyadic Pattern Assessment

| Pattern | Dynamics | Prognosis |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Complementary (D-S, W-W) | Stable but may feel "stuck" | Good if both satisfied; problematic if one resents their role |
| Anti-complementary (D-D, S-S) | Competitive or paralyzed | Unstable; needs negotiation of roles |
| Flexible/Shifting | Healthy alternation of roles | Best prognosis if genuine rather than chaotic |
| Rigid complementarity | One always leads, one always follows | Risk of resentment in the submissive partner; power imbalance |

### Confidence Calibration
- Agency assessment from conflict narratives is most reliable -- conflict reveals power dynamics
- Communion assessment from descriptions of partner distress is most reliable -- how do they respond when the other needs support?
- Self-report of style is less reliable than described behavior -- people may describe ideal self rather than actual self
- Cultural norms significantly affect both agency and communion expression -- high agency in one culture may be normative in another
- Gender norms interact with both dimensions -- account for socialization pressures

## Interaction / Decision Implications

### When Agency Is Too High (Dominant/Controlling)

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Validate their competence and capacity (they value it) | Challenge their authority directly (triggers escalation) |
| Introduce the concept of "influence" as more effective than control | Label them as "controlling" -- use descriptive language |
| Help them see the cost of dominance (partner withdrawal, resentment) | Submit to their framing in the consultation |
| Frame yielding as strategic strength, not weakness | Moralize about power dynamics |
| Explore what vulnerability looks like for them | Assume they're unaware of their impact |

### When Agency Is Too Low (Submissive/Passive)

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Support small steps toward assertion, not dramatic change | Push them to "stand up for themselves" all at once |
| Validate that their accommodating style has strengths | Frame submission as inherently pathological |
| Help them identify what they want (they may not know) | Assume they know what they want but can't express it |
| Practice assertion in low-stakes contexts first | Start with their most difficult relationship |
| Explore the fear underneath the deference | Interpret deference as simple weakness |

### When Communion Is Too Low (Cold/Hostile)

**Warmth is the master variable.** Research consistently shows that increasing communion is the single most effective intervention for improving relationships.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Approach without judgment -- coldness often covers vulnerability | Meet coldness with coldness |
| Look for the function of hostility (self-protection? hurt?) | Take hostile behavior at face value |
| Start with behavioral warmth targets, not emotional openness | Demand emotional vulnerability |
| Validate that distance can be protective | Imply that warmth = weakness |
| Model warmth in the consultation itself | Be clinical or transactional in response to coldness |

### When Communion Is Too High (Over-Accommodating)

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Validate their relational strengths | Dismiss their care as "codependency" |
| Help them see the cost of self-sacrifice (burnout, resentment) | Frame caring as pathological |
| Support boundary-setting as an act of care (for self AND relationship) | Suggest they care less |
| Explore whether their warmth is freely given or compulsive | Assume all warmth is healthy |
| Check for resentment underneath the accommodation | Take their "I'm fine" at face value |

### When Rigidity Is High

**This is the most important diagnostic signal from the circumplex.** A rigid style means the person applies the same approach everywhere, even when it's counterproductive.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Name the pattern gently: "I notice you describe a similar dynamic across several relationships" | Pathologize the pattern |
| Explore its origins and protective function | Demand immediate change |
| Introduce the idea of "expanding the repertoire" not "changing who you are" | Frame rigidity as character flaw |
| Start with small experiments in low-stakes contexts | Suggest dramatic style overhauls |
| Connect to attachment patterns if relevant (avoidant -> rigid agency; anxious -> rigid communion) | Ignore the underlying motivation |

### For Dyadic Interventions

**The complementarity principle is the key leverage point:**

1. Map both partners on the circumplex
2. Identify the "dance" -- what pulls what
3. Introduce awareness: "When they do X, you tend to do Y -- and that reinforces X"
4. Suggest small counter-complementary experiments: "What if, the next time they took charge, you gently offered your preference instead of deferring?"
5. Increase communion FIRST (warmth is the master variable), then adjust agency

**Key principle**: The circumplex provides a MAP, not a judgment. Every position has strengths and costs. The goal is not to be at the center (bland, undefined) but to have FLEXIBILITY -- the ability to move to different positions as the situation requires, rather than being stuck in one spot. Rigidity is the problem, not any particular style.
