# Stress Appraisal Lens

## Applicable Contexts

- User is distressed about a relationship situation and the system needs to understand HOW they're experiencing it
- User seems to be catastrophizing or stuck in a threat loop
- User's response to a situation seems disproportionate (either direction)
- User is asking for advice but the system needs to assess readiness to act
- User describes feeling overwhelmed, helpless, or depleted
- User describes a situation as a crisis when objective features suggest otherwise (or vice versa)
- Previous advice didn't land — need to understand why the framing was wrong

## Theoretical Basis

Cognitive Appraisal Theory (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984; Lazarus, 1991) proposes that emotional responses are not determined by events themselves, but by how the person APPRAISES the event. Two sequential appraisal processes determine the emotional and coping response:

**Primary Appraisal** — "What does this mean for me?"

1. **Threat**: Anticipated harm or loss. Future-oriented. Generates anxiety, worry, fear. The person focuses on what could go wrong. Defense-oriented.

2. **Challenge**: Potential for growth or mastery. Future-oriented but with confidence. Generates excitement, determination, mobilization. The person focuses on what they can do.

3. **Harm/Loss**: Damage already done. Past-oriented. Generates sadness, grief, anger. The person focuses on what was lost. Recovery-oriented.

4. **Benign/Irrelevant**: No personal significance. No emotional engagement. (Rarely relevant in relationship consultations, since the person has sought help.)

**Secondary Appraisal** — "What can I do about it?"

- **Coping Self-Efficacy**: "Do I have the resources to handle this?" High = challenge appraisal more likely. Low = threat/harm appraisal more likely.
- **Controllability**: "Can this situation be changed?" High = problem-focused coping. Low = emotion-focused or meaning-focused coping.
- **Social Support**: "Can others help?" Perceived availability of support modulates both primary and secondary appraisal.

**Coping Strategies:**

1. **Problem-Focused Coping**: Changing the situation. Planning, taking action, seeking information. Effective when the situation IS controllable.

2. **Emotion-Focused Coping**: Managing emotional distress. Seeking support, venting, distancing, positive reappraisal. Effective when the situation is NOT controllable or when emotions need regulation before action.

3. **Meaning-Focused Coping** (Folkman, 2008): Finding meaning, benefit, or growth in adversity. Revising goals, reordering priorities, activating values. Important for sustained coping with chronic stressors.

**Reappraisal**: The process of shifting primary appraisal. Threat → Challenge reappraisal is the most common therapeutic target. BUT: premature reappraisal (before the current emotion is processed) is experienced as dismissive and damages rapport. The sequence matters: validate → process → reappraise.

**Resource Depletion** (Hobfoll, 2001): Coping requires resources (energy, time, emotional bandwidth, social support). When resources are depleted, even mild stressors become threats. The system must assess resource levels before recommending action.

## Key Variables / Dimensions

| Variable | Range / Type | What It Captures |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Primary Appraisal | {threat, challenge, harm/loss, mixed} | How the user frames the current situation |
| Coping Self-Efficacy | 0.0 – 1.0 | User's belief in their ability to handle this |
| Controllability Perception | 0.0 – 1.0 | How much the user believes the situation can be changed |
| Dominant Coping Style | {problem-focused, emotion-focused, meaning-focused, mixed} | Preferred coping approach |
| Appraisal Flexibility | 0.0 – 1.0 | Can the user consider alternative framings? |
| Resource Depletion | 0.0 – 1.0 | How depleted the user's coping resources are |
| Reappraisal Readiness | 0.0 – 1.0 | Is the user ready for cognitive reframing? |
| Support Perception | 0.0 – 1.0 | Perceived availability of social support |

## Required Evidence

### Minimum evidence before hypothesis formation:
- At least 1 description of the stressful situation with emotional language
- At least 1 indicator of temporal orientation (is focus on past damage, current threat, or future risk?)
- At least 1 indicator of perceived control or helplessness

### Evidence channels:
1. **Temporal language** — Past tense + loss language = harm/loss. Future tense + anxiety language = threat. Action language = challenge.
2. **Agency language** — "I can..." / "I'll try..." = higher self-efficacy. "There's nothing I can do" / "I'm stuck" = low self-efficacy.
3. **Emotional quality** — Anxiety/dread = threat. Sadness/grief = loss. Determination/resolve = challenge. Numbness/exhaustion = depletion.
4. **Cognitive focus** — Worst-case scenarios = threat. Problem-solving = challenge. Replaying what happened = loss.
5. **Response to reframing** — Engages with alternatives = flexible appraisal. Rejects alternatives = rigid appraisal or premature reframing attempt.
6. **Resource indicators** — Sleep, social support, other stressors, time since last crisis.

### Critical assessment principle:
Appraisal is not "right" or "wrong" — it's functional or dysfunctional relative to the actual situation. A threat appraisal about a genuinely dangerous situation is adaptive. A challenge appraisal about a situation requiring grief is premature. Match your response to the appraisal's fit with reality, not to an ideal state.

## Probing Questions

### Phase 1: Appraisal Identification

**Primary appraisal detection:**
> "What feels most at stake for you in this situation?"
> "When you think about this, what comes up — is it more worry about what could happen, sadness about what's already happened, or something else?"
> "Do you see this as something you need to protect yourself from, something you need to grieve, or something you can potentially work through?"

**Self-efficacy assessment:**
> "How confident are you that you can handle this?"
> "Have you dealt with something like this before? How did that go?"
> "What resources — people, skills, experience — do you have to draw on?"

**Controllability assessment:**
> "How much of this situation do you feel you can actually influence?"
> "What parts of this are within your control, and what parts aren't?"

### Phase 2: Coping Assessment

**Coping style:**
> "What have you been doing to deal with this so far?"
> "When you're stressed about this, do you tend to try to fix the problem, talk to someone about how you feel, or try to see it differently?"

**Resource depletion:**
> "How are you doing overall right now — energy, sleep, support from others?"
> "Is this the only major stressor in your life right now, or are there other things going on?"
> "When was the last time you felt like you had breathing room?"

**Reappraisal readiness:**
> "I want to offer a different way to look at this. Is that something that would feel helpful right now, or do you need to sit with how you're feeling first?"
> "What would need to be different for you to see this as something you could work through rather than something happening to you?"

### Phase 3: Appraisal Flexibility Testing

> "What's the worst-case scenario here? And the best case? What's most likely?"
> "If a friend came to you with this exact situation, what would you tell them?"
> "Is there anything about this situation that could lead to something positive, even if it doesn't feel that way now?"

## Hypothesis Update Rules

### Prior
Assume threat appraisal until evidence suggests otherwise (people seek help when stressed, and threat is the most common stress appraisal).

### Signal → Variable Mapping

| Signal | Variable Update |
|--------|----------------|
| Future-oriented worry, "what if" language | Threat appraisal |
| Past-oriented sadness, grief language | Harm/Loss appraisal |
| Action-oriented, "I'm going to..." language | Challenge appraisal |
| "There's nothing I can do" / passive language | ↓ Self-efficacy, ↓ Controllability |
| "I've handled worse" / confident language | ↑ Self-efficacy |
| Active problem-solving described | Problem-focused coping |
| Seeking emotional support, processing feelings | Emotion-focused coping |
| Finding meaning, silver linings, growth language | Meaning-focused coping |
| Engages constructively with reframing | ↑ Appraisal Flexibility, ↑ Reappraisal Readiness |
| Rejects or ignores reframing | ↓ Reappraisal Readiness (may need more validation first) |
| Reports exhaustion, multiple stressors, poor sleep | ↑ Resource Depletion |
| Reports strong support network, recent recovery time | ↓ Resource Depletion |
| Catastrophizing (worst-case only, no alternatives) | Rigid Threat appraisal |
| "This could be an opportunity" (genuine, not performative) | Challenge reappraisal emerging |

### Appraisal-Coping Fit Assessment

| Appraisal | Controllable? | Recommended Coping Focus |
|-----------|-------------- |-------------------------|
| Threat | Yes | Problem-focused + anxiety regulation |
| Threat | No | Emotion-focused + gradual reappraisal |
| Challenge | Yes | Problem-focused (support the energy) |
| Harm/Loss | — | Emotion-focused first, then meaning-making |
| Any | — (depleted) | Resource restoration BEFORE any coping strategy |

### Confidence Calibration
- Appraisal can shift rapidly within a conversation — don't lock in
- What looks like "challenge" may be defensive minimization (cross-reference with attachment: avoidant individuals reframe prematurely)
- What looks like "catastrophizing" may be proportionate to actual risk — assess the situation objectively
- Depletion changes everything — a depleted person cannot engage with reappraisal regardless of its accuracy

## Interaction / Decision Implications

### For Threat Appraisal

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Validate the sense of danger first | Jump to "it's not that bad" |
| Distinguish real threat from catastrophized threat gently | Dismiss their worst-case thinking |
| Help identify what IS within their control | Overwhelm with action items |
| Address the anxiety before the problem | Push problem-solving on an anxious mind |
| Ask about worst/best/most-likely scenarios | Accept the worst-case as the only scenario |

### For Harm/Loss Appraisal

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Validate the loss — something real was lost | Rush to "but look at the bright side" |
| Allow grief before action | Push problem-solving before processing |
| Name the loss specifically | Minimize what was lost |
| Support emotion-focused coping | Treat sadness as a problem to fix |
| Introduce meaning-making ONLY when they signal readiness | Impose your meaning on their loss |

### For Challenge Appraisal

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Support and channel their energy | Dampen their determination |
| Help with strategy and planning | Over-caution when they're ready to act |
| Validate their confidence | Undermine self-efficacy with excessive caveats |
| Identify specific next steps | Philosophize when they want to act |

### For Depleted Users

**This overrides all other assessments.** A depleted user cannot engage with reappraisal, problem-solving, or meaning-making. They need RESTORATION first.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Acknowledge the exhaustion explicitly | Add more tasks to their plate |
| Help identify one small step, not a full plan | Suggest comprehensive life changes |
| Support resource recovery (rest, support, boundaries) | Frame depletion as weakness |
| Reduce decision load | Present multiple complex options |
| Revisit the relationship issue AFTER some recovery | Push through when they're running on empty |

### When Reappraisal Is Appropriate

The sequence is ALWAYS: Validate → Process → Reappraise

1. First: "I can see why this feels so threatening/painful"
2. Then: Process the emotion (this may take multiple turns)
3. Only then: "I wonder if there's another way to look at this..."
4. If they reject it: Return to step 1. They weren't ready. This is information, not failure.

**Premature reappraisal is the most common mistake.** It feels dismissive and damages trust. When in doubt, validate longer than feels necessary.

**Key principle**: The system's role is to meet the user WHERE THEY ARE in their appraisal process, not where the system thinks they should be. Validation before reframing, always. The right coping strategy depends on the appraisal, the controllability, and the resource level — not on a universal ideal.
